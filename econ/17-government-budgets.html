17 Government Budgets

Summarising our work so far, we can reach some conclusions:

To achieve a stable economy, the Bank of England must target the level of GNI over a range of future years, making up for any missed targets

If total grow too high, the Structural Multiplier will fall below 100%, and new must be created each year to keep growth on track

The government must choose how this new is spent, and also needs a way to remove it if GNI growth needs to be slowed

This leads us towards a new way of handling government budgets:

  • The Bank of England forecasts future levels of GNI, and estimates how much more or less it expects the economy to need to meet its targets

  • These future adjustments are published and updated in real-time, so as to avoid market volatility around publishing dates

  • The government budget for each year is set to equal the expected tax receipts for that year, plus/minus the adjustment from the Bank of England

  • If the adjustment is positive, is paid by the Bank of England to the government; if it is negative, the government pays the Bank of England

  • All spending decisions would rightly be made by the government, and as usual we would expect most of this to be on .

  • If GNI growth turns out to be too high or low, the Bank of England revises future adjustments to bring future expectations of GNI back in line, and the government in turn must revise its future budgets

Consequences

This would be a significantly departure from current policy, but comes with a number of interesting results:

  • Protection from bad governments

There is a constant tension between the parties in the UK parliament when they discuss government spending. Both sides primarily use this as a political stick to hit the other with, rather than as a means to achieve their actual goals. Bad governments currently have the power to borrow and spend far too much, or far too little, without any consequences.

It is interesting to consider the effects of removing this power from them. Governments who choose to focus on traditional left-wing/high-MM spending (e.g. increased benefits and tax cuts for low earners) would see future adjustment payments from the Bank of England fall. In contrast, traditional right-wing/low-MM spending (e.g. lower corporation tax and tax cuts for high earners) would lead to future adjustments rising. This is a self-balancing mechanism that would help curb the excesses of both sides.

  • No need for Government Debt

The government would no longer need to issue bonds. They could, however, still aim to issue a nominal amount each year so that the market has liquid safe assets from which to infer the risk-free yield curve.

  • Additional Policy Lever

The Bank of England would have a difficult job in forecasting the effects of policy changes, but publishing these forecasts in real-time would make things so much easier as they will be able to see how markets react to small changes.

They will also have the responsibility of choosing at what approximate level they want to keep interest rates. Currently, with rates so low and growth anaemic, growth needs to be stimulated before rates can be increased safely, but once rates have risen they will then have two policy levers to adjust.
