12 Monetary Policy

Monetary policy primarily acts by changing expectations of the future state of the economy.

Conventional

The Bank of England changes policy by adjusting the Bank Rate, which is the rate of Interest they pay on Currency deposited with them by commercial banks. This rate affects the economy directly via two mechanisms:

  1. A lower Bank Rate decreases the demand from banks for Currency, and increases their demand for Savings, raising their price. More valuable Savings make consumers and companies richer, temporarily encouraging them to spend more on Consumption.

  2. Although the Bank Rate doesn’t directly affect interest rates on Debt, a lower Bank Rate generally leads to lower market rates on deposits and mortgages as well. This encourages consumers and companies to borrow and spend, causing more Debt to be issued and increasing GNI.

Raising the policy rate has the opposite effects:

The Bank of England is in a uniquely powerful position within the economy, and market rates will often adjust without the Bank needing to do anything. If the Bank’s target is well understood, markets will move in advance of any changes to the Bank Rate, which can even lead to situations where the Bank Rate never needs to move in the first place.

Notice that raising the Bank Rate doesn’t actually decrease the amount of Debt, just discourages further increases. This asymmetry means that cutting then raising interest rates will not have zero overall effect, but will temporarily increase the total amount of Debt and slow GNI growth until it is paid back.

Problems

This approach has worked for a long time, but each of its two transmission mechanisms causes a positive feedback loop:

  1. Increases income indirectly by increasing the value of Savings. This is a back-to-front approach, and will mean incomes falling relative to total Savings, as only part of the increase is passed down. This means a falling share of GNI going to Wages, and a lower Structural Multiplier.

  2. Increases income by encouraging more Debt. As Debt slows GNI growth, even more Debt is then required to keep GNI on track. Interest rates have fallen consistently since the 1970s and couldn’t be cut any further during the 2008 financial crisis, resulting in a long recession.

These two effects mean that conventional monetary policy cannot keep working indefinitely.

Unconventional

Unconventional policy is actually much simpler: the Bank of England can increase or decrease the amount of Currency in circulation, either temporarily or permanently.

Temporary

Quantitative Easing (QE) is a way of achieving a temporary increase, by directly purchasing government bonds from existing bondholders.

  • The Bank of England buys government bonds in exchange for Currency
  • This reduces outstanding Debt and increases Currency
  • When the bonds redeem, their face value is paid to the Bank of England, removing it from circulation
  • The government replaces this with new Debt, thus returning us to where we started
  • Overall there is no net effect after the bond has matured

Change in distribution of assets from QE

While it is active, QE works primarily through it’s affect on the value of Equity and Debt. Raising the amount of Currency in circulation without changing demand for it will increase the value of Savings. This makes the holders of these Savings richer, temporarily encouraging them to spend more on Consumption . This exacerbates the Savings feedback loop caused by conventional policy.

In some countries, the central bank performs small-scale QE as part of conventional policy, to move short-term market interest rates towards their target rate

Permanent

A more sustainable way to achieve a similar effect would be for the Bank of England to print Currency and spend it on Consumption.

Additional Consumption spending directly raises GNI, increasing incomes relative to total Savings, and therefore Wages, the Structural Multiplier and GNI growth.

The value of Savings will also rise, but as this is a secondary effect driven by higher incomes, the overall impact would still be positive for the Structural Multiplier.

If the Bank of England is credible enough with its targets, the expectation of Currency being printed if required will help to keep GNI on track to meet those targets

However, this approach suffers from a very significant flaw: choosing where to spend the money is inherently political. It seems wrong to have an unelected central bank deciding where to target this, while the government and opposition argue about where tax rises and cuts should fall. This is also a solid criticism of QE, since it targets banks and bondholders. Are they really the most in need of help?

Furthermore, remember that our targets for GNI growth are symmetric - if growth is too high one year then we need a way to remove the new money from circulation. This is difficult for the Bank of England to achieve by themselves.

If new Currency is going to be injected into the economy, it should be spent on Consumption, and the government should be deciding how that money is spent, not the Bank of England.
