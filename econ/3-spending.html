3 Spending

Every time somebody spends part of their income on Consumption that money is split up into the following groups and paid onward:

  1. Wages
  2. Dividends
  3. Interest
  4. Consumption on intermediate goods

There is no Interest here, as that is only ever paid by the Bank of England

Example - Buying a hat

Money spent on the hat is paid to a shop, and then split as follows:

  1. Wages for the shop workers
  2. Dividends for the shop owners
  3. Interest for anyone who has lent money to the shop
  4. Consumption buying the hat from the manufacturer

The money spent buying the hat goes to the company who made the hat, and then split as follows:

  1. Wages for the workers who made the hat
  2. Dividends for the owners of the company
  3. Interest for anyone who has lent money to the company
  4. Consumption buying materials to make the hat

And the money spent on the materials gets split in the exact same way. If we continue until we run out of things to break down, we can sum all the component incomes and they will add up to the amount spent on the hat.

This demonstrates the simple fact that in a closed economy, any money spent on Consumption becomes income for somebody else.

Taxation

Conveniently, we can include tax without changing anything - Taxes will remove a percentage of Wages, Dividends and Interest and become a fifth group at each stage. These tax receipts will be split by the government and paid out as Wages of government employees, Interest on Government Debt and Consumption. The Taxes group is therefore very similar to a second Consumption group.

In future, when we refer to Consumption we really mean Consumption plus Taxes.

Gross National Income

If we add up the after-tax income of every person and company in the country in a single year, we get the Gross National Income (GNI) for that year. References to GNI from now on will mean nominal GNI, and real GNI will be referred to explicitly.
