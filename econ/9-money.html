9 Money

Money is what we use when we are exchanging one Asset for another. We call it the “Medium of Exchange.”

The fact that it isn’t one of the components of Wealth may initially seem confusing but it is a mistake to think of money as purely a stock variable when it is really a flow, the oil that keeps the economy running.

Assets can be money if we can exchange them for other Assets. There is no precise dividing line between money and non-money, so some Assets may be money only in some situations.

Examples

  • is definitely money, in all circumstances. It is also the “Medium of Account”, as prices are conventionally displayed in it.
  • Bank deposits are also money now that most transactions are made electronically, as long as the bank is safe (in the UK they are also guaranteed by the government up to £85k per person)
  • Short-dated government bonds are nearly money, as they trade in a liquid market so can easily be exchanged for deposits. Additionally their value is very stable and unlikely to vary much in the time it takes to sell them

Other assets are kind of similar to money but fall down in some ways:

  • Long-dated government bonds are equally as easy to exchange for deposits, but their value can change by a lot if interest rates move suddenly
  • Short-dated bonds from safe companies are less liquid and more risky than government bonds

And then other assets are not really like money at all:

  • Long-dated or high-yield bonds are more illiquid and more likely to change value quickly
  • Shares can change value dramatically from one day to the next
  • Houses are illiquid and slow to buy and sell

Sources of new Money

There are four main sources of new money:

  1. Private Debt
  2. Government Debt
  4. Foreign Trade

We can now use our understanding of Money, Assets and Liabilities to look at the effects these sources have.
