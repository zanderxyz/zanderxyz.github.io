16 Austerity

Austerity is the government policy of pursuing deficit reduction, with the aim of running a balanced budget, with no new Government Debt.

In the United Kingdom, the cost of the 2008 bank bailout and a fall in tax receipts caused Government Debt to increase from 40% of GDP in 2007 to 75% in 2010. This prompted the newly elected Conservative government to aggressively pursue deficit reduction via cuts to welfare programs and widespread public sector job losses, mirroring similar approaches in Southern Europe.

Alternatives to Government Debt

Applying our understanding of the different sources of new money, we can see that any reduction in the government deficit would slow growth unless this was replaced by another source of new money. There are three other sources that could have been chosen:

  1. Private Debt

This is a completely reasonable alternative that makes sense if you are a proponent of small governments. If one starts off with the assumption that government run programs are inherently inefficient, then one probably ascribes a lower Multiplier to government spending than the spending of private companies. This then suggests that cutting government spending while encouraging an increase in Private Debt might be an overall benefit to the economy.

There are, however, a few obvious problems with this approach:

  • Interest rates were already at all-time lows, and couldn’t be reduced any further.
  • The government spending that was cut was the spending with the highest Multipliers. Uunemployment benefit, for example, is virtually all spent by its recipients, so cutting it is a direct cut to GNI . Even if the government believed this would boost real growth in the long run, the damage to nominal growth in the short term was severe, and this was not made up elsewhere.

To increase Private Debt without reducing interest rates the government was forced to introduce policies to encourage additional mortgage borrowing. This further increased (already elevated) house prices, increasing the value of Savings .

  1. Foreign Trade

This can be thought of as the Germany approach. Germany runs the world’s largest Current Account surplus of roughly $300 billion per year (~8% of GDP). If you’re a country in that position, then there is no need to fuel GNI growth using new Debt of any kind.

It is, however, not possible to simply wish ones Current Account deficit will become a surplus and have it come true, and no steps were taken by the government to encourage a transition in that direction (which of course would take time, and only affect the long-term).

  1. Currency

Quantitative Easing was introduced by the Bank of England in 2009 once the Bank Rate couldn’t be cut any further. In 2011 and 2012, the programme was increased further in an attempt to offset the effects of reduced government spending.

Although somewhat successful in this regard, predictably this came about by further inflating the value of Savings , thus reducing the Structural Multiplier and moving us further away from a sustainable economy.

Short Term Thinking

We can see here how an ostensibly sensible policy can end up doing more harm than good if policymakers do not apply an appropriate level of critical analysis to their actions. Growth in the United Kingdom was almost certainly slowed significantly by austerity, due to the government focusing more on deficit reduction than growing the economy. We are left with higher house prices , an excess of Debt and lower real wages .

Indeed, governments across the world continue to focus on the short-term, to the detriment of the long-term prosperity of their countries.
