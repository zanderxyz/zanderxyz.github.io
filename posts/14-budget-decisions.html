14 Budget Decisions

Summarising the previous sections, we can reach some conclusions:

Targets for the level of GNI should be set for a range of future years

If total Savings grow too high, the Structural Multiplier will fall below 100%, and new money must be created each year to keep growth on track

Debt is a source of new money, and can give non-financial benefits, but slows future growth and lowers the Structural Multiplier

A Current Account surplus is a source of new money, but not all countries can simultaneously run one, and the United Kingdom runs a significant deficit at present

Currency printed by the Bank of England can ensure growth continues and also prevent a build up of Savings

The government must choose how this new Currency is spent, and also needs a way to remove it when growth needs to be slowed

This leads us towards a new way of handling government budgets:

  • The Bank of England forecasts future levels of GNI, and estimates how much more or less Currency it expects the economy to need to hit its targets

  • These future adjustments are published and updated in real-time, so as to avoid market volatility around publishing dates

  • The government budget for each year is set to equal the expected tax receipts for that year, plus/minus the adjustment from the Bank of England

  • If the adjustment is positive, Currency is paid by the Bank of England to the government; if it is negative, the government pays the Bank of England

  • If GNI growth turns out to be too high/low, the Bank of England revises future adjustments to bring future levels of GNI back in line, and the government in turn must revise its budget

Conclusions

This would be a significantly departure from current policy, but comes with a number of interesting results:

  • Protects people from bad governments

There is a constant tension between the parties in the UK parliament when they discuss government spending. Both sides primarily use this as a political stick to hit the other with, rather than as a means to achieve their actual goals. Bad governments currently have the power to borrow and spend far too much, or far too little, without any consequences.

It is interesting to consider the effects of removing this power from them. Governments who choose to focus on traditional left-wing/high-MM spending (e.g. increased benefits for low earners, tax rises on high earners) would see future adjustment payments from the Bank of England fall. In contrast, traditional right-wing/low-MM spending (e.g. lower corporation tax and income tax) would lead to future adjustments rising. This is a self-balancing mechanism that would help curb the excesses of both sides.

  • No need for Government Debt

The government would no longer need to issue bonds. They may, however, wish to issue a nominal amount each year so that the market still has liquid safe assets to infer the yield curve from.

  • Interest Rates stability

The Bank of England would have a difficult job in forecasting the effects of their actions, but publishing these forecasts in real-time would make things so much easier as they will be able to see how markets react to small changes.

They will also have the interesting choice of choosing at what approximate level they want to keep interest rates. Currently, with rates so low, they would need to print enough Currency to stimulate growth far enough that rates could be increased safely, but once rates have risen they will then have two policy levers to adjust.
