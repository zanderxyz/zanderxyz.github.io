Weâ€™ll start by defining three groups, which represent all assets that can be held, and a fourth group representing anything else that people may buy.

  1. Currency

All money created by the Royal Mint and Bank of England, it comes in both physical (banknotes and coins) and electronic forms.

  1. Equity

All other permanent assets: houses, shares & commodities such as gold.

  1. Debt

This represents Currency you are owed by somebody else. Usually the borrower will pay interest each year until they have to repay the original sum borrowed.

Money in your bank account is actually Debt with no fixed repayment date, and not Currency. Nowadays most exchanges of money are paid in the form of bank deposits rather than cash changing hands.

  1. Consumption

Anything temporary, such as services, food and drink, clothes, or intermediate goods used in the production of other goods. Although some of these are semi-permanent (such as clothes), we treat them as if they cease to exist after being purchased (since the benefits are purely non-financial, we exclude them from the model).

If a business invests in building a factory to increase their future profitability, the expenditure would count as Consumption, although it would increase the value of the company once complete. In contrast, if they buy an existing factory then that would be a direct purchase of Equity from the seller.
